Section 1: Research Setup

Research Question

The primary research question this analysis seeks to answer is to what extent do physical property characteristics (area and age) and location determine the variance in housing sale prices within the NYC real estate market?

Motivation

Buying a home has become such a far away ideal for most Americans, especially in an aggressive and fast-paced market such as NYC. As a soon-to-be college graduate planning to move to New York City, I am motivated to understand the mechanics of this market to one day navigate it effectively. By identifying what “justifies” a price; specifically the relationship between square footage, building age, and borough location, this analysis empowers buyers to distinguish between fair market value and an overpriced asset.

Section 2: Data

I am using the NYC Housing Prices dataset by Ishank2005 who sourced from public records published by the NYC Department of Finance and the NYC Department of City Planning. Each row of data is a house sold and its related information including borough, building type, and square footage among other things. These records are a sample of final sale prices in the year 2024 and 2025. The types of variables included in the data are structural and geographic, focusing on physical characteristics of the property and location.

Section 3: Hypotheses

  1. Being located in a borough other than Manhattan (Brooklyn, Bronx, Queens, or Staten Island) will be associated with a lower price compared to properties in Manhattan.
  2. Properties with higher building density (the building area divided by lot area) will be associated with a lower price.
  3. Properties that were built more recently will be associated with a higher price.

Section 4: Conceptual Understanding of OLS

Why OLS is Appropriate for this Analysis

OLS is a suitable modeling strategy for my research question because each coefficient will tell the specific value added/lost by that variable while holding all other factors constant. This will aid my understanding of the unique market drivers in NYC. OLS also provides residuals; a large negative residual identifies a “deal” (underpriced relative to its variables), while a large positive residual indicates an overpriced property.

My dependent variable (final sale price which will be logged) is appropriate for OLS because it will have a more normal distribution of residuals and turn coefficients from being a dollar amount to a percentage. This addreses how NYC real estate prices are right-skewed and better reflect differences across variables.

Key Assumptions of OLS

The linearity assumption means that we assume the 5th floor adds the same value as the 50th floor. It is not reasonable to assume linear relationships and I should empirically evaluate it.

My observations are not independent; if a park or a good school is nearby, residential property value will be higher than a residential property farther away from these amenities. Clustering and dependence are definitely issues in a densely populated city, houses in the same zip code likely has related errors since they share the same amenities.

Heteroscedasticity would mean my model is much better at predicting “regular” houses than “luxury” ones. If the spread of errors grows as the price grows, my p-values become unreliable.

The normality of errors assumption matters for inference because if my errors aren’t normal, then my p-values become unreliable as I will not have confidence that my independent variables aren’t just showing up due to a few extreme outliers.

If two variables are highly correlated it means that the model wouldn’t know which one to give the “credit” to for the price. If there is perfect multicollinearity, the model cannot be estimated.

Consequences of Violations

If the linearity assumptions is violated it is both biased and inefficient since the line is curved.

If the independence of observation assumption is violated, the model is inefficient and p-values are too small.

If there is heteroskedasticity then the model is inefficient and predictions are less reliable.

If there is multicollinearity, the model is inefficient since it can’t tell which IV to give “credit”.

Alternative Approaches

If OLS assumptions are violated, I can transform specific variables using log or squared, I can either combine or drop one of the variables, I can use robust standard errors, or different models like glm().

Section 7: Independent Variables (IVs)

Variable Selection

Key independent variables are:

  • yearbuilt

  • bldg_density (the building area divided by lot area)

  • borough_y (borough_x uses numbers while this variable uses abbreviations which help with readability)

Control variables are:

  • unit_bins (residential units put in categorical bins)

This is to prevent the model from assuming that more units means more money. A Brownstone is definitely more expensive than an apartment in an apartment building, bigger does not always mean better.

  • res_ratio (the residential area divided by building area)

This accounts for how much of the building is actually livable or hallways. Properties with smaller residential areas may have a yard or amenities like a gym.

Conceptual Meaning

The yearbuilt variable records the year the structure was officially completed and an older yearbuilt value should affect the price by decreasing it because of the wear on the structure; older pipes, roofs, boilers, etc.

The bldg_density variable measures the ratio of building square footage to the size of the land plot. Buildings with higher density may represent apartment complexes and, as stated above, single family homes (less density) are much more expensive than an apartment in an apartment building.

The borough_y variable records which borough the property is located in. This will affect the price because everyone wants to live in Manhattan which drives up the price.

For the control variable unit_bins, which categorizes buildings by the number of residential units, it affects the price by ensuring the model does not compare the price-per-unit of a skyscraper to the price of a private Brownstone.

For the control variable res_ratio, which measures the percentage of the building’s total area that is dedicated to residential area, this captures properties that are not only living spaces, but could also be office spaces, shopping areas, restaurants, or residential amenities which will drive up the price.

The yearbuilt variable is statistically significant and has a negative coefficient of 0.001. This means that, holding all other variables constant, for every one unit increase in yearbuilt (one year closer to current year), there is a 0.1% decrease in price. The log_density variable is statistically significant and has a positive coefficient of 0.157. This means that, holding all other variables constant, for every 1% increase in building density, there is a 0.157% increase in price. Substantively, this indicates that properties with more intensive land use (higher building-to-lot area ratios) are pricier.

The borough_y variables are all statistically significant and have a negative coefficient. This means that, holding all other variables constant, a property located in a borough other than Manhattan is associated with a ~50% decrease in log-price compared to the reference group of Manhattan. Section 10: Model Revision

Based on diagnostics, I will only evaluate observations with 12 <= log_prices <= 15 which removes non-representative transactions, such as familial $1 transfers and ultra-luxury outliers, which were causing heavy deviations in the Q-Q plot tails. I will create an interaction between the logged building density and the borough to specifically address the high correlation coefficient of 0.739 identified between these two variables in the correlation matrix. This allows the model to acknowledge that the impact of density is not uniform across the city; instead, the market value of building density is dependent upon the borough in which the property is located. I will also square yearbuilt which hopefully captures the non-linear relationship where price premiums are associated with both the newest construction and the highly-regarded ‘pre-war’ historic housing. To address the heteroskedasticity, I will use robust standard errors in my model to allow the variability of the error terms to vary by individual response, rather than assuming it is constant. This produces more reliable and valid standard errors.

Model Interpretation

The yearbuilt and yearbuilt2 variables are statistically significant. Yearbuilt has a negative coefficient while yearbuilt2 has a small positive coefficient. This means that the relationship is not linear and is concave up. Prices decrease as buildings get newer initially, but eventually, the trend reverses as ultra-modern construction and amenities start adding value again. Holding all else constant, the market values both the very old and the very new more than the “middle-aged” buildings.

The log building density coefficient is positive and statistically significant. For the reference borough (Manhattan), a 1% increase in building density is associated with a 0.472% increase in property price. This reflects the high market value placed on land-use intensity in the urban core.

The main effect for borough_y for Brooklyn, Bronx, Queens appear positive but represent the difference in price only when density is at its lowest point compared to Manhattan. The main effect for Staten Island is not statistically significant, meaning at the lowest density levels, there is no detectable price difference between Staten Island and Manhattan.

The density and borough interactions are all statistically significant and negative. Substantively, this means that high-density buildings in other boroughs are valued much less per unit of density than those in Manhattan.

The unit_bins100+ coefficient is negative and statistically significant. Substantively, compared to a single-unit property, a building with over 100 units sees a massive per-observation price discount. The market places a high premium on the exclusivity of small-scale residential living. Interestingly, Units: 5-10 is not significant, meaning there is no statistical difference in price between a 1-unit and a 7-unit building.

The res_ratio is statistically significant and negative. Hold all other variables constant, for every 1-unit increase in the residential-to-total area ratio (moving toward a purely residential building), price decreases by 10.4%. This indicates that properties with a mix of commercial space are valued more highly than purely residential ones.

The R2 value indicates that model explains 28.2% of the variance in New York City property prices.

The F-statistic is very large and statistically significant. This means that my independent variables explain significantly more variation in the data than the random errors.

Hypothesis Testing

I reject the null hypothesis of my first hypothesis that being located in a borough other than Manhattan (Brooklyn, Bronx, Queens, or Staten Island) will be associated with a lower price compared to properties in Manhattan. This is because all four interaction term coefficients are statistically significant, negative, and larger than just borough_y of which the statistically significant coefficients are positive. Since the interaction coefficients are larger, they offset just borough_y as log building density increases. The interaction terms confirm that once the building density is controlled, Manhattan remains the most expensive borough and properties in other boroughs are associated with comparatively cheaper prices.

I reject the null hypothesis of my second hypothesis that higher building density is associated with a lower price. This is because the coefficient is statistically significant and positive (0.472), not negative like predicted, indicating that higher (logged) density actually increases market value, instead of the predicted devaluation. Holding all other variables constant, a 1% increase in the building-to-lot area ratio is associated with a 0.472% increase in price.

I reject the null hypothesis of my third hypothesis which is that properties that were built more recently will be associated with a higher price because the statistically significant coefficients for yearbuilt and yearbuilt2 are not the same direction (negative and positive respectively) which proves that the relationship is not linear. This concave-up curve reveals that the market places a high premium on historic properties (the left side of the curve) and the newest modern developments (the right side), while “middle-aged” buildings see lower valuations.

Section 13: Limitations & Reflection

Some limitations of my analysis is that it can only explain less than 30% of variation in NYC housing prices, when the standard for a good model would be over 70%. Though the model can predict the average home sale price, it is especially not good at prdicting the outliers outside of the scope I gave it (12 <= log_price <= 15). My model also lacks variables that would affect the price, such as date of latest renovation, proximity to nearest park or nearest subway station or construction. A 1920s building that was gutted and renovated in 2025, which is worth far more than one with its original plumbing, yet they would appear identical to the yearbuilt variable. Being 500 feet from a subway entrance or Central Park can drastically increase a property’s price, but these small distances cannot be reflected in the borough_y variable. These amenities would certainly affect the price and therefore increase the R2 value, improving the model. This dataset also does not capture the volatility of mortgage interest rates since it only spans two years. Having 30 more years of data and its interest rates as a control variable, would improve the model.

In an ideal setting with access to richer datasets, the model would incorporate variables such as proximity to transit, parks, and grocery stores, quality of property views, renovation history, mortgage interest rates at the time of sale, and school district rankings. Furthermore, clustering observations by zip code or neighborhood could better capture geographic heterogeneity and improve the robustness of standard error estimation. Including these additional explanatory variables would likely account for a greater share of the unexplained variance in housing prices and could potentially raise the model’s R2 above 70%.
